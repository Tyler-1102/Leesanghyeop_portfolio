## The problem it started from

AI news and papers pour out every day, but nowhere answers the question "so what happens to **my job**?" AI RADAR automatically collects and analyzes news, papers, and GitHub data to show AI technology trends and their impact on occupations — and I owned **the prompt and data-structure design for the per-occupation AI impact analysis**.

## What I did

### Decomposing occupations into core tasks

Sweeping claims like "developers will be replaced by AI" are useless. I designed the data structure to decompose each occupation into core tasks and analyze AI's impact **task by task** — the same skeleton as job analysis in HR.

### Structuring automation scenarios, strengths, and skills

I designed an output schema so every task analysis comes back in the same shape: a step-by-step automation scenario, its expected impact, **the strengths that remain human**, **the skills needed next**, recommended tools, and the level of supporting evidence. That makes different occupations and tasks comparable against one standard.

### Evidence-based analysis

To keep the analysis from becoming plausible guesswork, I injected the past month's news keywords and research-field data into the prompts so the job-impact analysis stays **grounded in data**.

## Where this meets HR practice

Decomposing a job into tasks, structuring what gets automated and what stays human, and identifying which skills to build maps directly onto **HR's job analysis, competency modeling, and training-system design**. It was hands-on experience thinking about workforce planning in the AI era with data.
